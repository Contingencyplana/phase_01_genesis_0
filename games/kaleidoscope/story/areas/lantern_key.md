## Description

Lantern Key is a small, windworn island whose importance comes not from comfort or population, but from duty.

It exists as part of the archipelago’s working infrastructure: a place of beacon-keeping, weather-watching, and practical vigilance over nearby waters. Its pier, yard, tower path, and lookout all feel shaped by salt, wind, and repeated labor.

Compared with Brinehook Island, Lantern Key feels lonelier, harsher, and more exposed.

And yet, like the other islands, it stands close to something older than its current use.

---

## Why It Matters

Lantern Key shows that the archipelago is not only made of homes and harbors, but also of places that help other places survive.

It expands the world by introducing public duty, weather awareness, and the practical work of keeping routes safer for those who cross between islands.

It also deepens the recurring mystery by showing that the circle-and-dot symbol appears even here, faintly preserved in older stone beneath the island’s newer structures.

---

## Key Sites

### Lantern Key Pier

A rough landing point where boats come in under harder wind and more exposed water.

This is the first point of arrival on the island.

---

### Beacon Yard

A weathered working yard with oil casks, rope, tools, and signal equipment used to maintain the island’s lantern and warning systems.

This is the practical heart of Lantern Key.

---

### Tower Path

A climbing path shaped by wind and salt air, leading upward from the yard toward the lantern tower.

It gives the island much of its exposed, watchful character.

---

### Lantern Tower

A high vantage point overlooking nearby channels and sea lanes.

From here the surrounding waters feel connected, dangerous, and necessary.

---

### Rock Shelf Below the Tower

A more sheltered but older part of the island’s stone base, beneath or near the tower.

This is where the old foundation stone stands, bearing the faint worn trace of the circle-and-dot symbol.
